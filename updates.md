Another cool thing about the Sierra’s is the water crossings.  There are endless rivers, creeks and streams coming down the mountains so you eventually have to cross them.  And then 10 minutes later another one, then 2 hours later another one.  One strategy is to take all your footwear off, change into the crocs i have as camp shoes, cross, then towel off and put back on my hiking shoes.  This takes about 15-20 minutes per river. And no one has tome for that. So another strategy is to search out a fallen tree, or a nice series of rocks you can jump over that crosses the river and balance yourself across…this will have you walking upstream for 15-20 minutes in search of the perfectly fallen tree/rocks, which likely doesn’t exist and then you have to cross at an imperfect spot anyway…and no one has time for all that either.  So option 3 which everyone eventually gets to anyway, is you just walk across the river in your shoes and socks because they’re probably a little wet from walking on the snow from the high pass earlier anyways, so who cares if they get wet again.  Then you go to sleep at 11000 feet and your wet shoes and socks freeze overnight.  It makes cold mornings even colder putting wet frozen socks and shoes on.  But don’t worry - in 1 hour you’ll either be walking on slushy snow or crossing another river getting them freshly wet again.  Today I had a big choice to make - 1) follow the PCT and cross Evolution Creek(more like a gushing river), it can often be higher than waist deep…or 2) take an alternate path through a meadow where the water is less deep.  I didn’t come all the way up into the Sierra’s to take safe alternates, so I chose adventure through the deep water.  I unbuckled my waist belt and chest strap, in case the water swept me away, and waded towards the middle of the river- it was flowing fast and when you step, where you think your foot is gonna go, is actually about 2 steps away because the river is moving you downstream faster than you can put your foot down.  You just gotta go with it and realize you are not gonna walk straight across - you are walking downstream at a diagonal and the quicker you get across the less downstream you go.  Before I realized it I was waist deep in water but fully committed to crossing.  Luckily it didn’t get any deeper than that and I made it across the deepest river (actually just a creek) successfully.  It could have been much worse, but then again everything could always go much worse.

It’s been an eventful 4 days in the High Sierra.  This southern part of the Sierra’s is the heart of the PCT.  It has all the high passes over 10,000 feet, as well as Mt Whitney.  By the time you get to where I am, at mile 860 I won’t have to go over 12000 feet again for the rest of the trail.  After the next 100 miles I’ll barely be over 10,000 feet again.  Theres just 3 more “big passes” Seldon at 10,913 (tomorrow), Silver at 10778(in 2 days), and then the last big pass is Donahoe Pass at 11000 feet near Yosemite at mile 930, and that will close out the “High Sierra”.  Since Wednesday when I got back on trail I crossed Kearsarge pass at 11200 feet, the Glen Pass at 11957, the next day I did Pinchot pass at 12,127, then Mather Pass at 12094, and today I crossed Muir Pass at 11970.  Each one was eventful in their own way, and when your up above 10000 feet it’s hard to breathe, and you are dealing with ice and snow that is hard to navigate.  I definitely needed the ice axe and microspikes to get over these tall passes.  Yesterday when descending Mather Pass, the second most notorious of these passes, the popular way down was to Glacade(basically slide on your butt). Sadly I climbed this pass at 6 in the morning meaning everything was frozen solid.  As i started to glacade, I immediately picked up way too much speed in the ice and was quickly barreling down the steep hill at speeds where I could hurt myself, and I was still at the top of the mountain.  I had to self arrest and quick otherwise any bump and I’d start rolling with all my gear(not safe and not good!!!).  I had my ice axe next to my hip in glacade position to slow my speed, but when i dug it in it just jerked my whole body sideways, and next thing you know i was on my side totally out of control and picking up more speed.  I panicked and swung the ice axe as hard as i could into the mountain over my head.  The axe blade caught the ice, and immediately was pulled from my hand, but I had a wrist strap on, which pulled my arm and slowed me almost instantly.  You are not supposed to do this, as I found out, because you can dislocate your arm, which I probably wasn’t too far off from doing.  Luckily the ice was a little forgiving that day and the blade kind of cut through it rather than just stopping fully.  I then remembered you are supposed to roll onto the axe with your whole body so it doesn’t rip your arm off.    Sometimes you get lucky when learning new skills is all I can say.  After i stopped(self arrested) i was still laying on the side of a steep slope, so instead of sliding down i got back on my feet (and microspikes) and slowly kicked steps down the mountain.  It took forever, but was way safer than just letting momentum carry me.

   So the fun never ends on the PCT.  The day after Whitney you have to go over Forester Pass which is the highest point on the PCT at 13,150ft.  And it is notorious for the “Death Shute” at the very top where the snow covers the trail and you have to traverse, just like Whitney.  The trail up to Forester was an uphill slope for like 9 miles.  I kept looking at the mountains in front of me thinking where the hell was the pass?  There’s just no way to pass over the cliffs of the mountains.  Unfortunately i opted for beauty sleep again rather than leaving early and by 10:30 realized i may be too late to get across the snow if it’s too melted, or if i posthole on the approach.  Luckily it was again cold as heck today and the snow stayed firm.  As i got closer to the mountains i finally saw the switchbacks up the slope, and thought “No way”.  There looks like a nice pass a few mountains to the right, but John Muir chose the gnarliest little gap between mountains as the way to go.  I only managed about a mile an hour on the switchbacks as the elevation absolutely destroyed my lungs and heartrate.  I made it to the dangerous shute of death at about 11:45 and again was all alone.  All the smart folks left at like 2am to get nice firm snow and ice across the shute.  To do this traverse for some reason the guys who blazed the trail make you climb straight up ice for about 6 feet.  You absolutely can’t do this without microspikes, and good luck without an ice axe.  To climb the first steps i slammed my ice axe into the mountain and was so thrilled when it held firm into the ice.  I pulled myself up into the footsteps and just focused one by one to get across.  It was a little scarier than Whitney the day before because I knew, no one was coming behind me.  If I fell it would be at least 18 hours before anyone else showed up.  Once you get across the Chute theres a few more little switchbacks and then you have to use your axe again to pull yourself over the Cornice at the top.  I had so much anxiety over both Whitney and Forrester after the snow storm that when i made it to the Forester Pass sign i yelled as loud as I could for about 2 minutes.    There was no one to hear my victory screams, but I tried to make sure everyone on earth knew I had overcome the two biggest obstacles on the PCT in horrible conditions.  As scary as the climb up Forester is, the backside is like nothing.  I knew even if i postholed all afternoon i could still get down, but there was a 500 foot “Glacade” where you basically sit down and slide down the mountain.  The slope was so gradual that you just coast down to easy breathing.  I then just kinda walked straight down the mountain through the snow half skiing and half stepping in my trailrunners and microspikes. When you pass over Forester you are in the “Kings Canyon” and it is absolutely beautiful.  I think it’s more beautiful than Yosemite or the Grand Canyon.  The afternoon descent was so pleasant the forest was so beautiful and it was sunny and warm.  The “Kings” - all the mountains around you are stunning peaks - and the snow just makes them more beautiful.  You drop to 10,000 feet in just about an hour and then have these massive 3000 foot peaks all around you.   John Muir knew what he was doing when he made this trail, and I feel like my timing with the snow and summer approaching are perfect.  Usually this place is infested with mosquitoes, but i’m early enough that they aren’t really around yet.  Sometimes if you suffer through a crappy snowstorm you get rewarded with absolute beauty on the other side.  Today is another day I’ll never forget, for how difficult the climb was, the danger of the traverse, and the overwhelming beauty of Kings Canyon and the John Muir trail on the other side.
